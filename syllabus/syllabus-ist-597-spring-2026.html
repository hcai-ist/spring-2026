Description

This course will explore design principles and implementation strategies for Human Centered Artificial Intelligence (HCAI). It will focus on understanding requirements and guidelines for AI applications to support human values, ethics, and positive user experiences. The course will also explore the challenges and opportunities of using generative AI in different application contexts. By the end of the course, students should be able to conduct need assessments for designing AI applications, prototyping human-AI interactions, and human-centered assessment of AI applications. The course will follow a learning-by-doing approach with hands-on class activities and interactions.

Learning Objectives

  • Identifying societal challenges and opportunities of emerging AI applications
  • Assessing requirements, approaches, and goals for human-centered AI
  • Applying principles and methods for creating and evaluating human-centered AI systems

Course Requirements

Class Activity (30%)

We will conduct interactive discussion sessions based on the theme and assigned readings on a given week1. Each student will lead the discussion at least twice during the semester. Sign up for at least two classes as a discussion leader in this spreadsheet.

Readers

Readers will create one slide for each of the assigned readings:

  • Add your response to the corresponding slide deck in Google Drive
    • There is one folder for each class.
    • Each of these folders have one slide deck for each assigned readings
      • For example, Week 3 has two decks: one for Vaccaro et al. and another for Lee et al.
    • Create one slide per article
  • Respond in 1-2 sentences to the following categories
    • Contributions — What are the main contributions here?
    • Insights — What did you learn or find compelling?
    • Surprised — What challenged your expectations?
    • Test of Time — Will this still be true or relevant in 5 years?
    • Connections — How does this link to other topics, readings, or the world?
  • Add your slide 24 hours before the class, so that discussion leaders can include them in their activity

Discussion Leaders

The discussion leaders will synthesize responses from readers and their own thoughts from all assigned readings into an exploratory exercise for the class:

  • Each exercise should take 30–40 minutes and then another 30–40 minutes of reflection and discussion
  • The exercise should not just be a lecture
    • You should summarize the responses from the readers (< 5 minutes)
  • For designing interactive exercise, please use the following resources:
    • Stanford TeachingWriting Activities. E.g.,
      • Mining reading
      • Doubter and Believer
    • University of Waterloo In-Class Activities List. E.g.,
      • Three-step interview
      • Fishbowl discussion
      • Think-Pair-Share

Assignment (20%)

There will be two assignments throughout the course. See Canvas for deadlines.

Final Project (50%)

Each student will complete a final project. The main goal here is to learn to design, develop, and evaluate a HCAI system from scratch. As such, the project provides an excellent opportunity to put skills developed in the course into practice. In an ideal case, the project will become a part of a student’s research portfolio and resume for future internship and job opportunities. Your project paper could also be a first draft for a peer-review submission. We also highly encourage you to use this opportunity to participate in external events like Nittany AI Challenge and IdeaMakers Challenge.

There is considerable flexibility in terms of choosing a topic for the final project. You should consult with the staff as early as possible regarding a project topic.

Project Proposal (5%)

The proposal should provide a clear outline of your plan and timeline for the project. It should be around 2 pages and include:

  • What and why: You should provide a concise description of the problem you are trying to address and identify why this is an important problem to address. Who will be your intended users?

  • How: The proposal should describe how you are going to approach the problem. You should try to justify why this approach makes sense, perhaps indicating some references you will be using to develop the project. You should also identify your plan for implementation. For example,

    • What are the interactions and use cases you will aim to implement?
    • What types of data will you use?

  • Evaluation: What are the criteria and metrics for success for your project? How are you planning to evaluate your system?

  • Timeline: You should also provide a detailed plan and timeline for your project. What are the major milestones in terms of implementation? When and how will you complete these milestones?

Proposal Presentation (10%)

Each student will present their project proposal to the class. You should plan a 5-minute presentation of your proposal with the goal of presenting enough concrete detail to get feedback and suggestions on issues that are important to you as you are carrying out the project. During the presentation, you should clearly convey what the project is, what you are trying to achieve, the current state of the art and your planned contribution (what is new in your project).

Progress Report (5%)

The goal of the progress report is to get another set of reviews and feedback from the instructor. It will also help to identify whether you need to revisit the scope of the project and update the timeline accordingly. The report should include:

  • A concise description of the research contributions and goals
  • An introductory literature review
  • Description of data plan
    • what types of data will be used for your project
    • How are you going to get relevant datasets
  • Description of technology development and computational method implementation done so far
  • An updated project timeline (what has been done and what needs to be done)

Final Presentation (10%)

At the end of the class, students will do a poster presentation of their projects. Each presentation will be 5 minutes, supported by a single-page research poster. This is good practice for conference poster sessions, which are a common event at research conferences that often attract more junior-level researchers.

Final Code + Paper Submission (20%)

Your final submission should contain: code and a paper.

  • Code: Submit the code and a README file with instructions regarding how to run the code.
  • Paper: Please use the CHI Late-Breaking Work (LBW) format for the final paper:
    • Template: ACM Master Article Submission Templates (single column)
    • Length: Up to 8 pages long (excluding references)

Academic Integrity

Academic integrity is the pursuit of scholarly activity in an open, honest and responsible manner. Academic integrity is a basic guiding principle for all academic activity at The Pennsylvania State University, and all members of the University community are expected to act in accordance with this principle. Consistent with this expectation, the University’s Code of Conduct states that all students should act with personal integrity, respect other students’ dignity, rights and property, and help create and maintain an environment in which all can succeed through the fruits of their efforts. Academic integrity includes a commitment by all members of the University community not to engage in or tolerate acts of falsification, misrepresentation or deception. Such acts of dishonesty violate the fundamental ethical principles of the University community and compromise the worth of work completed by others.

Use of Generative AI tools

You are allowed to use generative AI tools for the assignments and the final project. Why? As you apply for jobs, you will need to know how to leverage AI tools to meet the expectations for both quality and timeliness. When using generative AI tools, you must show understanding of prompt engineering, engage in content generation checking, and appropriately reference the tool (e.g., ChatGPT, Midjourney) from which the content (e.g., text, image, ideas) was generated.

  • For suggestions on how to properly cite and reference generative AI tools, please see the following webpages:

    • https://apastyle.apa.org/blog/how-to-cite-chatgpt
    • https://guides.libraries.psu.edu/apaquickguide/generativeAI
    • https://dal.ca.libguides.com/CitationStyleGuide/citing-ai

  • For suggestions on how to write prompts that generate the most useful information, see this YouTube video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Gaf_jCnA6mc.

  • For a better understanding of how generative AI tools work and why checking is important, please check this YouTube video https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bZQun8Y4L2A.
